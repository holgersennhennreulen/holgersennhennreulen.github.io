Bayesian statistics is an analytical framework increasingly used in many different (most?) research areas.

But, why Bayes?

I don't know if Bayesian statistics is the only valid way of performing statistical inference - I believe I'd need a full-time position on the theory of science to maybe having a chance for answering this question.

R. McElreath on Deflationary statistics, Statistical Rethinking, p. 32:

"It may be that Bayesian inference is the best general purpose method of inference known. However, Bayesian inference is much less powerful than we’d like it to be. There is no approach to inference that provides universal guarantees. No branch of applied mathematics has unfettered access to reality, because math is not discovered, like the proton. Instead it is invented, like the shovel."
As this may not be fully convincing yet, Ben Lambert's 'Big World and Small World' at least convinces me:
"The Small World is still too big for our purposes. We usually have some knowledge about which areas of the Small World are nearest to T."
But it is nevertheless the way in which I want to perform statistical inference.

... and because of:

  • Bayesian interpretation of the probabilty concept.
  • Inclusion of one's current state of knowledge into the inference process by specification of a probabilistic distributon - called the 'prior'.
  • Inference process as updating one's prior by evidence coming from new data leading to a new version of the probabilistic distributon - now called the 'posterior'.
  • Post estimation calculations using samples from the posterior.
Bayesian statistics uses the language of probability to express our uncertainty about scientific questions by exploitation of the data we collect from the world to update our view on those scientific questions.
